What Aren’t We Being Told?

The media doesn’t always get it right. Most of the time, I don’t believe that’s intentional. Reporting is shaped by constraints most readers never see – limited access, legal barriers, fearful sources, incomplete information. Sometimes the full story simply isn’t available, no matter how hard a journalist tries to uncover it.

I learned this the hard way.

The Moment Everything Shifted

When I left a position in the late 2000s, my boss confronted me with a stark choice: “You can go along with our story, or you can tell your own and fight our communications machine.” I didn’t have the money to hire a firm. I didn’t have a platform or power. So my story never made it out into the world.

Major newspapers ran a story – just not mine. I had documentation – pages and pages of it. I even began writing a book once, hoping that maybe someday I could tell the truth in my own words. That book was never finished. But the documents followed me everywhere, packed into boxes and carried from home to home. Not because I expected vindication – but because they reminded me of something important: my truth existed, even if no one else ever heard it.

How It Changed the Way I See News

That experience changed how I consume the news. Now, whenever I read a headline or hear a story unfold, I find myself wondering: What am I not hearing? Who isn’t able to speak?

We often don’t know the answers. Not because journalists don’t care – but because there are many reasons the full story never makes it into the public eye:

  • Fear keeps some sources silent, even when conversations are “off the record.”
  • Others hesitate, knowing that telling one truth can invite scrutiny into every corner of their lives.
  • Some are bound by legal realities – pending lawsuits, internal directives, or concerns about perception.
  • Stories, much like data, are filtered through perspective and editorial judgment.
  • Public back-and-forths rarely illuminate the truth; they tend to entrench competing versions of it.
  • And hovering over all of this is a fundamental power imbalance: once a story is out, there is no rewind.

The Questions We Should Be Asking

I share this not to undermine the media, but to invite curiosity. To encourage all of us – readers, listeners, leaders – to ask better questions.

What else might be true?

Whose voice is missing?

And what risks might they face if they spoke?

Why Stories Are Never Complete

Communication is a powerful strategic tool. But silence can be strategic, too – and sometimes necessary for survival. So don’t take everything you read or hear at face value. Not because it’s wrong, but because it may be incomplete.

You may never hear the other voices.

But there is always more to the story.
